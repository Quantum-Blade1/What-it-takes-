ok, lets get back so as i said for answering this we need to understand some constraints

- 1. fundamentals to learing a programming language : india is producing cs engineers at mass scale, everyone knows that, but majority of them can't clear a basic technical interview and the reason is not that they r dumb its that the foundation was never clear/solid, clg teaches u to pass exams not to think like an engineer and self learning without direction just adds more confusion on top of a weak base, in 2026 everyone is vibe coding and building stuff with AI (including me) and thats fine but the difference between two people using the same AI tool is insane, the one who actually understands the domain uses AI to go 10x faster, the one who doesnt understand anything just gets a project that works but cant explain a single line of code, so the question is not whether to use AI or not its whether u have enough understanding to use it meaningfully, and that only comes from actually spending time with the fundamentals even when its boring even when it feels slow, u can't shortcut this part

- 2. vibe coding : look i vibe code too so im not here to pretend im above it, but theres a version of vibe coding thats dangerous and most people are doing that version, when u use AI to build something u dont understand ur not building a project ur building an illusion, and that illusion feels great until someone who actually knows the stuff starts asking u questions abt it, interviewers especially senior ones can tell within 5 mins whether u built something or whether u just prompted something, and the worst part is the time u spend "building" these AI coded projects feels productive but its actually just "cosplaying as a developer", ur effort is real but the learning is zero, vibe coding should be a tool for people who already know things to move faster not a replacement for actually learning, if ur using AI bcs u dont want to understand the hard parts then u r just delaying the moment where that gap becomes a real problem

- 3. courses/tutorials/tech influencers : biggest trap i feel, u watch a 10hr course on youtube feel like u r the best programmer ever and the next day u forget 80% of it bcs u never actually used it, and tech influencers r the worst part of this whole ecosystem they sell u the idea of "learn X in 30 days" or "i built X in 1 week" and u start believing that learning is supposed to be fast and flashy, but real learning is boring and slow and uncomfortable and nobody makes content abt that bcs it doesnt get views, courses r fine as a starting point but if ur entire learning is based on following someone else's roadmap then ur just copying a path not building one, the real skill is figuring out wt u need to learn next by urself

- 4. getting to multiple domains : this one is closely connected to point 1, people jump from web dev to DSA to ML to system design to devops all in 6 months and call it "exploring" but its actually just avoiding depth, depth is uncomfortable bcs it means staying in one place long enough to actually struggle and most people leave right before the struggle becomes growth, the market doesnt reward someone who knows 10 things at 20% each, it rewards someone who knows 2-3 things at 80%+ and can actually build or solve real problems, breadth is fine for curiosity but if ur trying to get hired or actually be good at something then pick ur lane and go deep, u can always explore later once u have a strong foundation in something

- 5. wt others do : this is probably the most dangerous habit in the cs community rn, everyone is checking wt others r building posting on linkedin "just shipped X in Y days" and u start comparing ur chapter 1 to someone else's chapter 10, and then u panic and switch domains or start a new project or follow a new trend, the truth is majority of those people posting those things either built it with AI and dont fully understand it (ref point 2) or they have been grinding for years and ur just seeing the highlight reel, stop optimising ur learning around social validation and start optimising around genuine understanding, the most dangerous question in ur career is "wt are others doing" and the most important one is "do i actually understand what i just built"

- 6. project quality vs project quantity : everyone is obsessed with having 5-6 projects on their github but nobody talks abt whether those projects are actually good or actually understood, one project where u genuinely know every decision u made, every bug u fixed, every hardwork u considered is worth more than 10 cloned tutorial projects or AI generated repos, when an interviewer asks "walk me through this" u should be able to talk for 20 mins without stopping, if u cant then it wasnt really ur project, build less but own it completely

- 7. consistency over intensity : everyone wants to do a "100 days of code" challenge or a "i will study 10hrs a day" sprint and then burn out in week 2, learning doesnt work like that, ur brain needs repetition over time not information dumped in bulk, 1 hour everyday for 6 months will beat 10 hours a day for 2 weeks every single time and not even close, the people who actually get good at this stuff are not the ones who had the most intense sessions they r the ones who just kept showing up consistently even on the days where they felt like they learnt nothing

- 8. communication and articulation : this is the most underrated skill in tech and almost nobody talks abt it, u can be a genuinely good engineer but if u cant explain what u did and why u did it then ur invisible, interviews are not just about whether u can code they r about whether u can think and communicate ur reasoning clearly, writing also matters, good documentation good comments good PR descriptions all signal that u r someone who thinks carefully not just someone who ships assign task fast, invest in being able to explain ur work as much as u invest in doing the work

conclusion :
look the point of everything written above is not to make u feel like ur doing everything wrong, its to make u aware of the patterns that feel productive but actually keep u stuck at a single point, the cs space in india especially is full of noise right now, everyone has an opinion on wt u should learn next wt framework is dying wt skill will get u hired, and in the middle of all that noise its very easy to lose track of the one thing that actually matters which is whether u are genuinely getting better or just looking like u are

the difference between someone who gets good at this and someone who stays average for years is not talent or luck or even the resources they had access to, its just clarity and consistency, clarity on wt they are actually trying to build towards and consistency in doing the unglamorous work every single day even when there is no visible progress

vibe coding is fine, courses are fine, exploring domains is fine, none of these things are the enemy, the enemy is doing all of them without intention, without understanding, without asking urself "am i actually learning something here or am i just staying busy"
so before u start the next course or build the next AI project or switch to the next domain just ask urself one question "if someone took away all the tools and all the tutorials right now, how much do i actually know?" that answer will tell u everything abt where u actually are and wt u actually need to do next
and that is a question worth sitting with
